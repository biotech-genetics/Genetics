Abstract

Land and farming are impacted by climate change in numerous ways, such as variations in the average annual rainfall, temperature, heat waves, weed growth, etc. Low rainfall and high temperatures have become important factors limiting the growth of salt-sensitive plants. Over US$12 billion is lost in agricultural productivity each year worldwide on land damaged by salt, and the amount is continually growing. Given the limited range of genetic diversity in salt tolerance within traditional crops, stress tolerance genes and mechanisms must be identified in extremophiles and then introduced into traditional crops. Because halophytes flourish in high-salinity conditions, they are considered extremophiles Haplophytes are extremely salt-tolerant plants that play an important role in protecting ecosystems due to their remediation abilities and their role as a reservoir of salt-tolerant rhizobacteria that could enhance the growth of crops under salinity stress. In order to compare plants that naturally grow under salt stress and plants that grow under normal conditions on the DNA level. DNA was extracted from Phragmies communis as a haplophyte weed sample that had been collected from Salines in Alexandria as well as from agricultural lands and subjected to PCR using three ISSR markers to differentiate between samples. Results had been recoded, and a dendrogram was generated, revealing that there are distinct differences between weed grown naturally on agriculture land and those grown on Salines.
