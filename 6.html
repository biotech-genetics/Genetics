Abstract

Soil salinity poses a significant obstacle to global food security, particularly affecting tomato crops. Previous studies have explored the use of nanoparticles to alleviate salinity stress in plants. However, limited research has been conducted on tomato plants using copper nanoparticles (CuNPs) coated with mint and moringa plant extracts. This study investigated the impact of these nanoparticles on tomato plants under salinity stress. After synthesizing and validating the nanoparticles, different concentrations (3, 6, and 9 mg/ml) were applied to tomato seedlings under salt stress (100 mM NaCl). Physiological and molecular analyses revealed that the foliar application of these nanoparticles improved tomato plant performance. Notably, the treatment with mint nanoparticles at a concentration of 3 mg/ml showed the most considerable improvements, including significantly increased shoot length and chlorophyll content, and decreased leaf yellowing compared to the salinity control group. At 6 mg/ml, the average moisture content increased 1 unit higher than the water only control group. Similarly, the treatment with moringa nanoparticles at a concentration of 3 mg/ml showed promising results, with decreased leaf yellowing and increased root length and chlorophyll content (9.1 units higher) compared to the salinity control group. The average moisture content increased 0.69 units higher than the water only control group. Moreover, RAPD-PCR analysis using 2 sets of random primers revealed some genetic variation between plants, indicating different responses to the treatments. These findings suggest the potential of medicinal plant extract-coated nanoparticles in mitigating salinity stress in crops like tomatoes, thereby enhancing food security.
