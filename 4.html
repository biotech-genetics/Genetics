Abstract

Wildlife genomics is a fascinating field that combines genetics, genomics, and conservation biology to understand the genetic makeup of wild organisms' populations. This research is a pioneering piece of work aiming to establish a solid groundwork for this field in Egypt. The goal of this research was to study extremophiles in the scope of wildlife genomics. Various aspects were covered, including phylogenetic tree analysis, biomolecule modeling, biochemical pathway analysis, and biotechnology applications. The workflow started with DNAfingerprinting of local extremophiles; including DNA extraction and RAPD-PCR, serving as a gateway to the investigation of their phylogenetics, which gave precise results and divided them into logical clusters (animals and insects, bacteria, and plants). Then, bioinformatics tools were used for non-local extremophiles, by using their GenBank retrieved DNA barcoding sequences to construct phylogenetic trees. Moreover, bioinformatics tools were used for biochemical pathway analysis of extremophiles, studying some of their principal genes, proteins, and enzymes, that help them survive the extreme conditions. As for completing the puzzle, extremophiles novel protein modeling was applied to enhance the understanding of how these protein structures function under such extreme conditions. Finally, the research article investigated the biotechnology applications potential of extremophiles conducting numerous experiments on their valuable bioproducts and utilizing some of them in agriculture, environmental and natural cosmetics applications. The holistic approaches and tools used in this research article enhanced the genetic and biochemical understanding of extremophiles, elucidating the factors that render them highly resilient, enabling their use for various biotechnology applications, as well as using them as genetic reservoirs to enhance the tolerance of other organisms to severe conditions due to climate change.
