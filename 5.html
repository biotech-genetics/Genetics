Abstract

Climate change and global warming have led to global drought, affecting food security. Scientists are exploring plant extract-coated nanoparticles to protect crops like tomatoes (Solanum lycopersicum) from drought stress. Our study aims to examine the effects of mint and moringa-coated nanoparticles on drought-stressed tomato plants. We prepared and confirmed these nanoparticles using techniques like TEM, SEM, and FTIR analysis. The nanoparticles were applied as a foliar spray at different concentrations (3, 6 and 9 mg/ml) on drought-stressed tomato seedlings induced by 10% PEG, along with control groups. We conducted physiological and molecular characterization, including measuring chlorophyll content and RAPD analysis on the treated plants. Physiologically, the concentration of 3 mg/ml mint nanoparticles showed positive effects, with no leaf yellowing observed. The root length and shoot length reached 21.1 and 19.3, respectively, the highest among all treatments. The lost water content was around 4.1 control's average of 4.4, and the chlorophyll value was approximately 44.3 higher than the no drought control. For moringa nanoparticles, the concentration of 6 mg/ml was most effective. It reduced yellowing leaves to 0.2, and the root and shoot length reached 17 and 16 respectively. The lost water content was 4.2, similar to the no drought control, and the chlorophyll value was about 42.7, half a unit lower than the no drought control. Moreover, RAPD PCR analysis revealed genetic variation between treatments, indicating different responses to the treatments. These findings suggest the potential of plant extract-coated nanoparticles in mitigating drought stress in crops like tomatoes, offering a promising avenue for enhancing food security amidst climate change challenges .
